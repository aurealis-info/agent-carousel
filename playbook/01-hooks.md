# Hooks Playbook

The slide-1 hook is the only element that decides whether a scroll stops. Apply this playbook by judgment, not regex matching.

## Pattern-interrupt principles

The audience is in rapid, distractible scrolling. Halting the scroll requires severe interruption.

- **Schwartz's 5 levels of awareness** — Unaware, Problem-Aware, Solution-Aware, Product-Aware, Most Aware. Social audiences exist almost entirely in Unaware or Problem-Aware. The slide-1 hook articulates the audience's latent pain, jolting them into Problem-Aware. Never lead with the product or the solution.
- **Hormozi's Hook-Retain-Reward** — Pattern interrupts rely on extreme specificity, weaponized recent macro-trends, or "manufactured" extreme scenarios. Levers: competence, curiosity, connection.
- **Naval-grade word economy** — "Clarity cuts through clutter." Philosophical brevity + high signal-to-noise ratio outperforms density.
- **Welsh's sub-niche specificity** — Hooks speak to a granular audience identity. "Smart people fail at marketing" beats "people fail at things."

## Frameworks adapted for slide 1

- **PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solution)** — Slide 1 is ONLY the problem. Forces a swipe to reach Agitate.
- **BAB (Before-After-Bridge)** — Slide 1 is ONLY the Before state. Vivid, specific depiction of the user's current negative reality.
- **AIDA** — Slide 1 is ONLY Attention. Push Interest and Desire to slides 2-3.
- **4 P's (Picture-Promise-Prove-Push)** — Slide 1 does Picture + Promise simultaneously via typographic hierarchy. Prove and Push live deeper.

## Curiosity gap formula

**Specificity + Novelty + Skin-in-the-Game.**

- **Specificity** — Concrete data, not vague platitudes. "Stole my $10k morning routine" not "stole my morning routine."
- **Novelty** — Counter-narrative information that disrupts the user's worldview. Dopamine spike from the unexpected.
- **Skin-in-the-game** — Demonstrates personal financial, emotional, or reputational risk tied to the claim. Authority through stake.

State the "what" and the "why." Withhold the "how." Forces the swipe to close the cognitive loop.

## Word economy — the ≤6 word rule

Hooks ≤ 6 words dominate save-rates. Reasons:

- The human eye uses too many saccadic movements to parse a dense paragraph during high-speed scroll. Dense text → abandon.
- A strict 6-word limit forces elimination of weak adjectives/adverbs. Distills copy down to powerful nouns + kinetic verbs that fit in foveal vision.
- Carousels get 1.9× higher reach than single-image posts, but that multiplier requires high swipe-through. Bad slide-1 hook destroys the multiplier.

## Identity-driven hook scaffolds

In niches where the audience's *identity* is the entry point (faith, masculinity, recovery, parenthood, founder, athlete, artist), five hook scaffolds outperform generic curiosity-gap formulas. Each locks a register (tone, self-selection, format fit) while leaving copy free, so the writer is solving for substance, not skeleton.

1. **Numbered habits** — `[N] things a [identity] [does | refuses | carries] [time | context]` → "5 things a godly man does at 5 AM." Anticipation + collectability. Highest save-rate scaffold.
2. **Identity + worldly aspiration** — `How to grow in [identity virtue] *and* [worldly value the audience already wants]` → "How to grow in faith and in your career." Bridges the curious into the substantive.
3. **Reframe** — `What [authority source] actually says about [topic the audience already cares about]` → "What Scripture actually says about ambition." Reframes a hot topic through the brand's authority source.
4. **Identity imperative** — `How a [identity] [verb] [object]` → "How a godly man handles success without losing himself." Self-selecting, declarative.
5. **Counter-frame** — `Why a [identity] [doesn't | isn't] [false archetype the audience has been sold]` → "Why a godly man isn't an alpha." Highest-leverage and highest-risk: directly confronts a competing frame, accepting hostile comments from the rejected audience as a feature.

These work because identity-driven niches don't have a Schwartz problem-aware ladder — they have a *whose-team-am-I-on* gate. The hook's first job in those niches is self-selection, not curiosity.

## Audience self-selection — the 1.5-second window

In identity-driven niches, the hook must claim the right audience inside ~1.5 seconds, or impressions are wasted on viewers who will never convert and the right viewers scroll past because they didn't recognize themselves.

Two valid mechanisms:

- **Mode A — bake the identity word into the hook itself.** "5 things a *godly* man does at 5 AM" self-selects in one word.
- **Mode B — short hook (2-4 words) + one-line subhead that does the self-selection work.** "Hidden men rot." → subhead "Why hidden Christian men rot — and free ones don't. James 5:16."

Failing to self-select is a category of hook failure that doesn't show up in the universal anti-patterns list but is fatal for identity-driven brands: viral reach on the wrong audience, under-claim of the right one.

## 5 anti-patterns — auto-reject

1. **Vague Promise** — Lacks quantifiable metric or specific outcome. Fails to trigger the Reticular Activating System.
2. **List Bait** — Generic listicle trap. Signals low-value commodity content not worth saving.
3. **Fake Urgency** — Fabricated scarcity. Destroys credibility, triggers ad-blindness.
4. **Generic "5 Things"** — Broad categorization without sub-niche focus. Fails Welsh's specificity mandate.
5. **"I used to..." Over-Share** — Trauma dump without immediate pivot to actionable thesis. Makes the creator the hero instead of the reader.

## Example hooks — graded

**GOOD (high conversion probability):**

```
1.  "The 2-hour workday is a lie."          — Contrarian novelty + brevity; attacks hustle myth.
2.  "Steal my $10k morning routine."         — Specific metric + skin-in-the-game + kinetic verb.
3.  "Stop writing emails like this."         — Direct command + curiosity gap; self-verification.
4.  "Your 401k is mathematically broken."    — High-stakes problem + objective tone.
5.  "Why smart people fail at marketing."    — Targeted identity + apparent paradox.
6.  "The silent killer of creative agencies." — Dramatic tension + sub-niche specificity.
7.  "Design portfolios that actually get hired." — Desired outcome + pragmatic tone.
8.  "I lost $40k learning this lesson."      — Skin-in-the-game + vulnerability + authority.
9.  "How to bypass the HR algorithm."         — Specific systemic pain + actionable promise.
10. "Delete these 3 apps today."              — Urgency without hyperbole + brevity + verb.
```

**BAD (algorithm rejection probability):**

```
11. "How to improve your life."               — Vague promise; no niche or mechanism.
12. "5 things successful people do daily."   — List bait clichéd.
13. "You need to read this right now!"        — Fake urgency without anchor.
14. "My journey to becoming a CEO."           — Self-indulgent over-share.
15. "Here are some tips for business."        — Zero specificity; no curiosity gap.
16. "What I learned about marketing."         — Passive voice; no authority.
17. "The ultimate guide to everything social." — Hyperbolic; impossible to deliver.
18. "I used to be so sad, but then..."        — Trauma dump without educational pivot.
19. "Tips and tricks for 2026."               — Generic; no novelty.
20. "Do you want to be rich?"                 — Scam-adjacent; immediate rejection.
```

Apply each anti-pattern as a principle, not a wordlist. A hook's vibe matters more than literal word matches.
