# Conversion Playbook — Single CTA: Download an App

Every carousel converges on one kinetic action: **download <App Name>**. This playbook governs how to bridge informational content into that download without feeling like a sales pitch.

## Friction-reduction language

Vague high-bandwidth requests induce massive cognitive load and immediate abandonment. Friction-reduction transforms abstract high-commitment asks into specific micro-commitments.

- "Download the free 12MB app" beats "Get started today."
- The former defines: exact payload, financial cost (free), digital weight.
- Removes decision anxiety — the user doesn't have to guess what happens after the click.

## The Minimum Viable Next Action (MVNA)

The user must be presented with the **smallest, most frictionless** kinetic movement to progress down the funnel.

- "Buy a $297 course" to a cold audience = proposing marriage on a first date.
- For app downloads, the MVNA is a single link or a single comment trigger.
- **Strip all secondary requests on the final slide.** No "save this post," no "tag a friend," no "let me know what you think below."
- Multiple CTAs trigger decision fatigue. Reducing to one clear path boosts conversion by up to **266%**.

## Bridging informational content → download

The CTA must feel like a logical escalation of the educational content, not a bait-and-switch. Position the app as the **automated, frictionless version of the manual framework just taught**.

Template: "You now understand [protocol]. But executing [protocol] daily requires [friction]. <App Name> automates [the friction]."

Avoid:
- Aggressive sales rhetoric.
- Feature-list dumps unrelated to the carousel's thesis.
- "But that's not all..." infomercial energy.

## Caption hook architecture (the 125-char rule)

Instagram truncates captions after **~125 characters**. Behind "Read more" the rest is hidden. The visible 125-char block is a critical secondary hook.

**Required structure:** `[High-value statement] + [Immediate kinetic command]`

- The high-value statement is typically the slide-1 hook or its trigram.
- The kinetic command is: "Comment X to get Y" OR "Download <App Name> in bio" OR similar imperative.

Example: "Manual tracking kills progress. Comment DOWNLOAD to get the automated tracker app instantly."

The strategist's caption.first_125_chars MUST contain the hook trigram (3 consecutive content words from slide 1's headline) AND a kinetic command verb.

## Comment-trigger pattern (SPARK method)

The "Reply X to get Y" pattern exploits Instagram's algorithmic prioritization of direct messages ("sends per reach"), a top-tier ranking signal. Triggers bypass the friction of "link in bio."

SPARK criteria for the trigger word:
- **S**pecific — the keyword is non-generic (e.g., "DECIDE" not "INFO").
- **P**romise — the triggered asset has clear, undeniable utility.
- **A**ction — single imperative verb on the user's side.
- **R**elevant — keyword reflects the carousel's thesis.
- **K**eyword — short, memorable, easy to type.

**When it works:** Triggered asset is genuinely valuable (the actual app download link, an exclusive PDF, a private workshop replay).

**When it cheapens:** Triggered asset is low-value (a generic checklist, a public link they could've gotten elsewhere). Conditions audience to view brand as spam → ignore future triggers.

## Conversion anti-patterns — reject

1. **Competing CTAs** — Asking for like + save + comment + follow + download simultaneously dilutes urgency and causes choice paralysis.
2. **Passive Framing** — "Link in bio if you want to check it out" instead of "Download <App Name>."
3. **Visual Clutter** — Overcrowded final slide with logos, URLs, QR codes, and text blocks.
4. **Abrupt Pivots** — Introducing a product feature on slide 10 entirely unrelated to slides 1-8's thesis.
