# Voice Playbook

The strategist and vision critic apply this playbook via JUDGMENT, not via regex matching. Brand-specific hard-reject wordlists live in each brand's `brief.yaml -> voice.banned_words`. The categories below are universal anti-pattern PRINCIPLES — apply them by reading the writing's tone and intent, not by looking up words.

## Voice modes

The strategist picks one of three modes per carousel. All three are universal — applicable to any vertical (fintech, fitness, productivity, recovery, design tools, education, philosophy, entertainment). The brand brief sets a default; the strategist may override per topic with justification in `creative_strategy_notes`.

1. **Guide** — Voice of a peer who has been there. Pragmatic, direct, slightly colloquial, deeply protective. Skin in the game. Converts on younger demographics seeking mentorship without condescension. Avoids parental lecturing.

2. **Expert** — Voice of clinical instruction. Highly structured, data-driven, unflinchingly authoritative. Specific metrics, named principles, named effects. Converts in B2B, finance, SaaS, advanced productivity, technical verticals.

3. **Storyteller** — Voice of experiential narrative. Self-aware, narrative-driven, lowers defenses through shared experience before introducing the mechanical solution. Converts on self-improvement, health, wellness, recovery, behavior-change verticals.

A brand's brief picks a `default` mode as a light identity anchor. The strategist may pick any of the three per topic; deviation should be justified in `creative_strategy_notes`.

## 5 universal anti-pattern categories — applied as principles

For each category: name + principle + reasoning + a few illustrative examples ("examples typically include..." — not exhaustive). Apply by judgment, not regex.

### 1. Pseudo-spiritual leverage

**Principle:** Framing practical advice as spiritual reward, mystical alignment, or divine economy.
**Why it kills credibility:** Transforms pragmatic content into spiritual leverage that signals scam-adjacent positioning to modern audiences. Even sincere readers detect the mismatch between practical-action content and mystical-reward framing.
**Examples typically include:** "blessing," "manifest," "the universe rewards," "abundance," "faithful," "divine alignment." Apply the principle: any framing that promises mystical/spiritual outcomes for practical action.

### 2. Synthetic intimacy

**Principle:** Manufacturing closeness through parasocial language as if the reader were already a close friend.
**Why it kills credibility:** Modern audiences identify the patronizing-friend voice as a marketing tactic and reject the brand as inauthentic.
**Examples typically include:** "hey bestie," "let's dive in," "I see you," "hey friends," "between us girls/guys." Apply the principle: any false-familiar framing that presumes intimacy the reader did not consent to.

### 3. Empty influencer-speak

**Principle:** Vocabulary that sounds aspirational but contains zero quantifiable specificity.
**Why it kills credibility:** Signals a void of actual intellectual capital — the writer has nothing precise to say so they reach for inflated abstractions.
**Examples typically include:** "game-changer," "level up," "crush it," "unlock your potential," "next-level," "10x your X." Apply the principle: any phrase that promises a transformation without a measurable mechanism.

### 4. Hustle-burnout culture

**Principle:** Vocabulary that valorizes exhaustion, sleep deprivation, or self-violence as the price of success.
**Why it kills credibility:** Modern audiences are actively rejecting burnout-as-virtue. The lexicon ages content immediately and signals 2010s influencer thinking.
**Examples typically include:** "grind," "bossbabe," "10x," "optimize your life," "sleep when you're dead," "rise and grind." Apply the principle: any framing that treats burnout as the cost of greatness.

### 5. Therapeutic jargon dilution

**Principle:** Replacing concrete actionable processes with feeling-vocabulary that obscures measurement.
**Why it kills credibility:** Sounds soft and sophisticated but communicates nothing operational. Reader can't act on it.
**Examples typically include:** "hold space," "the journey," "alignment," "honor your truth," "lean into." Apply the principle: any phrase that converts a measurable behavior into an unmeasurable feeling.

## Voice tests (creative-director heuristics)

Apply BEFORE finalizing copy. Failing any test = revise.

1. **The Subtraction Test.** Remove every adjective from the sentence. Does the sentence still communicate power? If yes, pass. If the sentence collapses, the original was padded — revise.

2. **The Peer Test.** Imagine a seasoned industry peer reading this. Would they roll their eyes? If yes, the writing leans on hyperbole instead of specificity — revise.

3. **The Algorithm Test.** Does this sound like a generic LLM summarizing a top-ranking blog post, or like a human with 10 years of skin in the game? Generic = revise.

## Masculine vs. feminine tone calibration

Tone calibration requires linguistic precision without crude stereotyping.

- **Masculine-calibrated tone** — relies on imperative verbs, spatial metaphors, language of conquest or architecture. Examples: "Build the framework," "Command your schedule," "Execute the protocol."
- **Feminine-calibrated tone** — relies on collaborative frameworks, relational nouns, language of stewardship, preservation, or integration. Examples: "Preserve your focus," "Integrate the system," "Protect your bandwidth."

Both retain absolute, unshakeable authority. Different psychological levers; same goal: drive the download CTA.

## Authority without preaching

Establish authority through OBJECTIVE DATA, not aggressive imperatives.

- Preachy: "You need to stop wasting time."
- Authoritative: "Manual data entry consumes 14 hours of an average workweek."

Present the reality; let the reader reach the conclusion. The CTA download becomes a logical necessity, not a mandated command.

## 5 voice anti-patterns — auto-reject

1. **Jargon stuffing** — using complex terminology to mask absence of insight.
2. **Fake vulnerability** — fabricating a minor flaw to appear relatable.
3. **Absolute commands** — "you must wake up at 5 AM" applied to lifestyle choices.
4. **Passive voice** — "the app can be downloaded" instead of "Download the app."
5. **Forced engagement tropes** — "Drop a 🔥 if you agree" appended to sophisticated copy.
