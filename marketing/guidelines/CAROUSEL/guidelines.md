# Carousel Guidelines

Carousels are the format ETHOS leads with. They're the depth play — the post a man saves, returns to, screenshots, and sends to a brother. Get this format right first; everything else is downstream.

For the general layer (pillars, voice, anti-patterns, bridge philosophy), see [`../content-guidelines.md`](../content-guidelines.md).

---

## The Mission

**Provide real value to the viewer first. Hook them through that value. The app reveal must feel earned, never pitched.**

A carousel that doesn't help a man before it asks anything of him is a broken carousel — no matter how clever the design. The quality of the free piece is the only honest argument for the paid one. If the value is real, the app reveal becomes a question the viewer is already asking ("what's this app?") rather than an ask we're forcing.

This is the entire game.

---

## Intention

Every carousel must be **extremely valuable to the viewer first** — useful enough on its own that even if he never downloads ETHOS, the post still stands as a piece of formation he'd want to keep.

Value precedes any product mention, always. The viewer's emotional arc through the post should be:

1. **Stop** — because the hook hits him.
2. **Stay** — because the value is real, specific, and applies to his actual life.
3. **Save** — because he wants to come back to it.
4. **Wonder** — what the app behind this is. If the *free* piece is this useful, the inside must be worth being in.

The carousel isn't a sales pitch with value tacked on. It's value that *implies* the app.

## Structure: The 5-Slide Chassis

Every carousel uses the same 5-slide arc. The arc is non-negotiable; the words and patterns inside each role are open.

| # | Role | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Hook** | Stop the scroll in under a second. No app mention. |
| 2 | **Value Tip #1** | Deliver real, specific value. Build trust. No app mention. |
| 3 | **Value Tip #2** | Deepen the value. Build emotional momentum. No app mention. |
| 4 | **App Tie-In** | Natural transition: the value just shown is what ETHOS does daily. First app mention. |
| 5 | **CTA + Screenshot** | Clear ask + proof of the product. Screenshot tied to slide 4. |

### Slide-Level Rules

**Slide 1 — Hook + Faith Signal**
- **Hook:** 2–6 words. Pain point or curiosity gap, not the topic name. No product, no app name, no promise of help — just the line that makes a man pause.
- **Faith signal — mandatory.** Slide 1 must self-select for *the man trying to grow in his faith*, not just any masculine viewer. Two delivery modes (pick whichever serves the line better):

  **Mode A — Bake the faith signal into the hook.** Use a faith-coded word (godly, faith, Scripture, the Word, God, Christ, Christian, Bible, Proverbs, etc.). The faith vocabulary self-selects in under a second. Five hook scaffolds work strongly in this niche — each is a *frame*, not a verbatim line. The bracketed variables are where the writing happens.

  | Scaffold | Skeleton | Pairs with |
  |---|---|---|
  | **Numbered habits** | `[N] things a godly man [does / refuses / carries] [time / context]` | Format A — Listicle |
  | **Faith + worldly aspiration** | `How to grow in faith *and* [worldly value the audience aspires to]` | Format E — Wisdom-Applied |
  | **Scripture-says reframe** | `What Scripture actually says about [topic the audience cares about]` | Format E — Wisdom-Applied |
  | **Identity imperative** | `A godly man [verb] [object/context]` or `How a godly man [verb]` | Most formats |
  | **Counter-frame** | `Why a godly man [doesn't / isn't] [worldly archetype the audience has been sold]` | Format E — Cultural Lens (highest leverage, highest risk) |

  Don't shoehorn the faith word — reach for the one that fits the topic. The model should write the specific line for the specific topic; the scaffolds exist to lock the *register*, not the *copy*.

  **Mode B — Keep the hook short and add a subhead.** When the hook's power comes from being 2–4 words and a faith word would crowd it ("Hidden men rot." / "She can smell desperation."), drop a single sentence beneath that does the self-selection work. The subhead can name a verse, the audience ("for the man trying to walk with God"), or the substantive frame — whatever does the work cleanest.
- Without a faith signal on slide 1, the hook can pull the wrong audience (generic self-improvement, Tate-adjacent, "alpha" curious) and under-claim the right one in the 1.5 seconds the thumb gives you.
- Visual must arrest within 3 seconds (see *Hook Engineering* below).

**Slides 2–3 — Value**
- Under ~40 words per slide.
- Genuinely useful. The viewer should be able to *apply it before he closes the app*.
- Second-person ("you").
- No app mention — the viewer is here for the value; respect that contract.

**Slide 4 — App Tie-In**
- One natural transition that connects what the viewer just learned to what ETHOS does daily.
- First mention of the app name. Conversational, not salesy — the value already did the work.

**Slide 5 — CTA + Screenshot**
- One specific CTA pointing to the in-app surface slide 4 named.
- Screenshot showing the feature referenced.
- ETHOS wordmark.

## Format Archetypes

Different ways to fill the value slides (2–3) within the 5-slide chassis. Each archetype has a different psychological mechanic and pillar fit. Mix them across the week — same pillar shipped in different archetypes keeps the feed alive.

### A — The Listicle ("X traits / X verses / X disciplines")
- The numbered promise. Cover slide names the count, each value slide names one item.
- Mechanic: anticipation + collectability — the viewer wants to know all X.
- Best pillar fit: Scripture Applied · The Vision · The Grind.
- Strength: highest save-rate format because it reads as a reference asset.

### B — The Mistakes List ("X things men get wrong about…")
- Diagnostic format. Each value slide names a common error + the correction.
- Mechanic: identification + relief — viewers recognize themselves and feel relieved someone named it.
- Best pillar fit: Real Talk · The Grind.
- Tone: never sneering. Direct, brotherly. The error is named because we've been there too.

### C — The Uncomfortable Questions
- Each value slide poses a question the viewer can't answer comfortably.
- Mechanic: self-confrontation. Saves and shares spike when a man wants to think on it later or wants someone else to wrestle with it.
- Best pillar fit: Real Talk · The Vision.
- Use sparingly — too many in a row reads as nagging.

### D — The Problem / Solution Comparison
- Early slides show the hard, manual, or wrong way. Later slides show the better path.
- Mechanic: contrast — the viewer feels the friction of the old way before the new way is offered.
- Best pillar fit: The Grind · Scripture Applied.
- Slide 4 lands hard here — the app *is* the better path the viewer just felt the absence of.

### E — The Cultural Lens
- Frame ETHOS through a cultural reference the audience already inhabits (a film, a book, a generational moment, a niche aesthetic the ICP already loves).
- Mechanic: borrowed attention. The viewer scrolls in for the culture and stays for the substance.
- Best pillar fit: any — the lens is the wrapper, the pillar is the meat.
- **Caveat:** the lens must not contradict ETHOS's voice. No edgelord references, no nihilist humor, no anything that wins the wrong audience.

## Hook Engineering (Slide 1)

The first 1–3 seconds is the entire post. If slide 1 doesn't stop the scroll, slides 2–5 don't matter. Tools to reach for:

- **Three-Word Rule.** The carousel's value prop must be capturable in three words or fewer. If it isn't, the topic is too diffuse — tighten it. Three words is the read time before the thumb decides.
- **The Pause.** Slide 1 must visually *or* verbally arrest in under 3 seconds. Visual contrast, layout disruption, an unfinished thought, a question the viewer can't answer without scrolling. The pause is the pre-condition for everything else.
- **Avoid the command frame.** Telling someone what to do triggers resistance. Frame the hook as *recognizing a pattern they're already in* or *escaping a negative they already feel*. Description over prescription. Mirror before you direct.
- **Specificity beats generality.** Concrete pain > abstract pain. Concrete moments (the Tuesday at 3 PM, the 2 AM scroll) beat universal categories (struggle, temptation). Specificity is what makes a man think *"that's me"* in under a second.
- **Self-selection.** A great hook makes the *right* viewer feel claimed and the wrong viewer keep scrolling. Don't try to make everyone stop; make the right man stop hard.
- **Faith signal on slide 1.** Self-selection has to specifically claim *the man trying to grow in his faith.* Either bake a faith-coded word into the hook itself (godly, faith, Scripture, God, the Word, Christ) or add a one-sentence subhead under the hook that does it. A great masculine hook with no faith signal pulls a generic masculine audience — that's the wrong room. (See slide 1 rules above.)

## Copy Psychology (Across Every Slide)

What hook engineering does for slide 1, copy psychology does for slides 2–4 — keeps the viewer moving and identifying. Patterns to reach for:

- **Identification before prescription.** Describe the viewer's reality on slides 2–3 before naming what to do about it. He has to feel seen before he'll accept direction.
- **Conviction-bait, not rage-bait.** Strong reactions drive saves, shares, and comments. ETHOS earns those reactions through *conviction* (truth that lands hard) — never through outrage, baiting, or triggering for the sake of it. Same psychological pull, different moral root.
- **Concrete > abstract — every slide.** "When she pushes every button you have" beats "in conflict." Real situations beat categories. This isn't just for the hook.
- **One idea per slide.** Don't crowd. The viewer is reading on a phone in 4 seconds — give him one thing to take away.
- **The aphorism close.** The last line of slide 3 should be screenshottable. Something he wants to send to a brother. That's the line that travels.
- **Earned scripture.** A verse lands when the slide *built up to it* — not when it's dropped on top. The application sets up the verse; the verse becomes the inevitable.

## Visual Relevancy

The "Eggs Theory" (Nick Weber): visuals that work belong to the world the viewer is *already trying to live*. Cheap, familiar, instantly readable. Aspiration through recognition, not fantasy distance.

Translated for ETHOS:

- The visual must read as *a man like me, on a day like mine* — not a polished influencer in a curated set.
- Aspiration through closeness — a Bible at 5 AM, a man on a quiet walk, a stone wall — not a fantasy of the man he can't become.
- If a visual makes the viewer think "I could be in that frame this week," it works. If it makes him think "I'd never be there," it doesn't.
- Cinematic *texture* (grain, depth, low light) beats high-budget set design. ETHOS is grounded; the visual language should feel grounded.

## Engagement Mechanics

Comments are the conversion signal. When a viewer asks *"what's the app?"* or *"how do I do this?"*, the carousel has done its job. Tools to drive that intent:

- **Comments > likes.** Optimize copy for comment intent. A line that makes a man want to push back, agree hard, or tag a brother is doing more work than a line he just likes.
- **Conviction-bait, not rage-bait.** (Repeated because it's load-bearing.) Strong takes that name a real tension drive comments without trading the brand. Cheap provocation kills trust.
- **Comment-trigger CTAs.** Asking the viewer to *"comment [word]"* (which auto-DMs them the link via ManyChat or equivalent) is the highest-converting CTA on Instagram for app installs. People are lazy about jumping to the App Store; a DM with the link removes the friction.
- **Soft CTA on viral posts, hard CTA on small posts.** Counter-intuitive but real: when a post is going viral (broad audience), under-mention the app — most viewers aren't your ICP and you'd burn the algorithm signal. When a post is small (500–1,000 views, all niche), be direct — those are the right 500 people.
- **Always-on watermark.** Every carousel must carry a subtle ETHOS mark — even on slides 1–3 where the app isn't named. The watermark is what attaches the brand to the value retroactively when a viewer goes looking.

## The Bridge

Slide 5's CTA must land on a *specific* in-app surface — Daily Anchor, Saved Verses, Streak, Chat Companion, Onboarding identity anchors, Weekly snapshot. Whichever surface slide 4 references, slide 5 closes on. Generic "check out the app" breaks the conversion mechanism.

The bridge is the contract: the value the viewer just received is *one rep* of what ETHOS does daily. Slide 5 makes that promise concrete.

## Production: Batch, Don't Boutique

Carousels are a volume game. The strategy is templates + variation, not one-off masterpieces.

- **Templates over one-offs.** Design a strong frame once, fill it with new content many times. The chassis stays; the substance rotates.
- **Modular variation.** Change one variable per version (hook, value, image, accent color). Don't redesign — re-spec. This is what the *3 versions per slide* rule enforces.
- **Shots on goal.** Volume + variation > polish. Even a great post can flop on a bad day; the only reliable way to find winners is to ship enough variations that some hit.
- **Native-looking text.** When a post is published, prefer pasting text in the IG/TikTok editor over baking it into the image. Native text reads as a real user to the platform algorithm; baked text often reads as ad and is throttled.
- **Re-cut winners.** A carousel that hit at 30 days can be re-cut at 60 and 90 — same words, new visuals — and ride for another quarter. Carousels are evergreen in a way reels aren't (see *Longevity* below).

The full production loop runs through the [`ig-carousel-creator`](../../aurealis_marketing_skills_simon/ig-carousel-creator/SKILL.md) skill: copy first (hard approval gate) → Pinterest reference image → Stitch generates 3 versions per slide → user picks finals.

**Don't open Stitch until copy is approved.** Redesigning slides is expensive; rewriting words is free.

## Success Metrics

| Metric | What it tells you | Target |
|---|---|---|
| **3-second retention** | Did the hook hold? | 75–80% |
| **Save rate** | Is this reference content? | >5% |
| **Save : like ratio** | Resonant or just "nice"? | >25% |
| **Carousel completion** | Did the arc hold all the way to slide 5? | >60% reach last slide |
| **Comment intent** | "What's the app?" / "How do I do this?" mentions | The leading conversion signal |
| **Share rate** | Did it trigger identification strong enough to send? | >2% |

If a post lands a high save rate, repurpose its template. If a post lands a high like rate but low saves, it was entertainment — don't double down.

### Longevity (the carousel's edge over reels)

Unlike reels, a strong carousel can pull views for **6–12 months** as an evergreen marketing asset. This is why batched production pays compound returns: a single winner in month 1 is still working in month 11. Track top performers and re-surface them on quiet weeks.

## Carousel-Specific Anti-Patterns

- **App named before slide 4.** Breaks the value-first contract. The viewer feels marketed-to early and the trust collapses.
- **Slide 4 that lectures or sells** ("here's why ETHOS is great"). The value already sold; let the tie-in be quiet.
- **Generic CTA on slide 5.** "Check out the app" is not a CTA. The CTA must point to a specific in-app surface tied to the post's content.
- **Mismatched slide 4 ↔ slide 5.** The screenshot must back up the tie-in line. If slide 4 names Daily Anchor, slide 5 shows Daily Anchor.
- **Value that isn't actually useful.** Generic advice ("stay disciplined", "trust God") trains viewers to skip ETHOS posts. Every value slide must make a man think *"that's actually helpful."*
- **Rage-bait or cheap provocation.** Outrage drives comments but trades the brand. ETHOS earns engagement through conviction, not contempt.
- **Hooks that command** ("Stop X", "Do Y") with no setup. Triggers resistance. Mirror before you direct.
- **Polished influencer aesthetics** in the visuals. The eggs theory: visuals must read as the viewer's world, not a fantasy he can't enter.
- **Verses dropped without setup.** Scripture lands when slides built up to it; not when it's pasted on a slide as decoration.
- **Stuffing more than 5 slides.** The arc breaks. If the topic needs more, it's two carousels.
